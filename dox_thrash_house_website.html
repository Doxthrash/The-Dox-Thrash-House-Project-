By Community Research Team | June 10, 2025

When we talk about Dox Thrash's revolutionary contribution to art, we're not just talking about technique—we're talking about power. The Carborundum printmaking process that Thrash co-invented in the 1940s wasn't just an artistic breakthrough; it was a direct challenge to the gatekeeping that had kept working-class artists, particularly Black artists, locked out of fine art production.

Traditional intaglio printmaking required expensive copper plates, specialized presses, and access to well-equipped studios—barriers that effectively excluded most artists of color from participating in what was considered "serious" printmaking. When Thrash and his colleague Michael Gallagher developed the Carborundum process, they created a method that could produce rich, textured prints using affordable materials and simpler equipment.

But Thrash didn't just democratize the technical process—he revolutionized the subject matter. His prints documented the daily life, struggles, and dignity of working-class Black Philadelphians at a time when such imagery was largely absent from gallery walls. Works like "Cabin Night" and "Morning Sun" weren't just beautiful—they were political statements about whose lives deserved artistic attention.

This is why preserving the Dox Thrash House and securing historic district designation for Sharswood matters. We're not just preserving a building—we're preserving the legacy of an artist who understood that innovation without liberation is meaningless, and that true revolution happens when we change both how art is made and who gets to make it.

By Housing Justice Committee | June 8, 2025

Let's address the elephant in the room: "Will historic designation raise my property taxes?" It's a legitimate concern, and we're not going to sugarcoat the complexities. But the research shows that community-controlled historic designation can actually protect long-term residents from the tax pressures of gentrification.

Here are the facts: In Philadelphia, properties in local historic districts are eligible for tax abatements and credits for rehabilitation work. The city offers a 10-year property tax abatement for substantial improvements to historic properties, and homeowners can access grants and low-interest loans specifically for historic preservation work.

More importantly, historic designation provides regulatory tools to prevent rapid neighborhood change that typically drives up property values and taxes. When developers can't demolish historic buildings for luxury condos, it slows the speculation that fuels displacement.

We're also organizing around community benefit agreements that would ensure any increased property values benefit current residents, not just incoming developers. This includes advocating for senior property tax freezes, expanded homestead exemptions, and community land trust options.

The real threat to affordability isn't historic designation—it's uncontrolled development and speculation. Historic designation gives us tools to fight back.

By Dr. Sarah Johnson, Community Historian | June 5, 2025

The Pyramid Club wasn't just where Philadelphia's Black elite gathered for dinner parties—it was where they planned the strategies that would reshape the city. Founded in 1937 at 1517 Girard Avenue, the club served as headquarters for a generation of African American professionals who understood that social change required both individual excellence and collective action.

Members included doctors, lawyers, teachers, and business owners who had broken through barriers to achieve professional success, but who recognized that their individual achievements meant nothing without community liberation. The club's mission—"cultural, civic and social advancement of Negroes in Philadelphia"—wasn't about assimilation; it was about building power.

The club provided crucial support to artists like Dox Thrash, offering both financial backing and a network of collectors who understood the political importance of supporting Black cultural production. When Thrash needed materials for his experimental printmaking, when he needed exhibition space, when he needed an audience that understood the revolutionary potential of his work—the Pyramid Club provided that foundation.

But the club also incubated the civil rights organizing that would explode in the 1960s. Members were early supporters of the legal challenges to segregated education that would eventually lead to Cecil B. Moore's Girard College campaign. They funded voter registration drives, supported economic boycotts, and provided the professional credibility that legitimized more militant organizing strategies.

When the club closed in 1963, it wasn't because the work was done—it was because the movement had moved beyond the confines of elite organizing into the streets, into mass action, into the kind of community-wide mobilization that historic district designation represents today.

By Sharswood Tenants Union | May 30, 2025

Gentrification is not a natural disaster. It's a policy choice, and historic designation can be part of the solution if we do it right. Around the country, communities have used historic designation as a tool FOR residents, not against them—but it requires organizing and community control from day one.

In Savannah's Victorian District, residents organized to ensure that historic designation came with tenant protections and affordable housing requirements. In D.C.'s Dupont Circle, community organizations used historic status to block luxury developments that would have displaced longtime residents. In San Antonio's Eastside, historic designation became the foundation for a community land trust that keeps property permanently affordable.

Here in Sharswood, we're building on these models. Our historic district proposal includes community benefit agreements that would:

  • Require any new development to include 30% affordable units
  • Establish right of first refusal for longtime residents when properties are sold
  • Create a community-controlled fund for home repairs and preservation
  • Mandate community review of any development projects
  • Protect existing tenants from displacement during renovation

But community benefit agreements only work if the community is organized to enforce them. That's why we're building tenant unions, homeowner associations, and neighborhood councils that can hold developers and the city accountable.

Historic designation without community power is just aesthetics. But historic designation WITH community power can be a foundation for the kind of development we want—development that honors our history while building our future.

Dox Thrash: Master of Print

Born in 1893, Dox Thrash emerged as one of America's most innovative printmakers, co-inventing the revolutionary Carborundum printmaking process that transformed the art world. His home at 2340 Cecil B. Moore Avenue became not just a residence, but a creative sanctuary where artistic innovation flourished.

Thrash's work captured the essence of African American life in Philadelphia, documenting the social and cultural fabric of our community with unprecedented artistic sophistication. His prints, characterized by rich textures and profound emotional depth, earned him recognition in major museums and collections nationwide.

The Dox Thrash House stands today as a testament to artistic excellence and community resilience, representing the broader cultural significance of the Sharswood neighborhood as a center of African American artistic and intellectual life.

Sharswood Through Time

1850s-1870s

Founding Era

Sharswood emerges as a residential neighborhood with distinctive Philadelphia row houses. The area develops into a community that will become central to Philadelphia's African American history.

1860s

Octavius Catto & Baseball Integration

Civil rights pioneer Octavius Catto organizes the Philadelphia Pythians, one of the first Black baseball teams. Catto fights for desegregation of streetcars, schools, and sports, laying groundwork for future civil rights victories.

1893

Dox Thrash Born

Dox Thrash is born in Griffin, Georgia, beginning a journey that would eventually lead him to become one of Philadelphia's most celebrated artists and a resident of Sharswood.

1910s-1930s

Great Migration & Community Building

During the Great Migration, Sharswood becomes a destination for African Americans seeking opportunity in Philadelphia. Black churches, community organizations, and cultural institutions establish deep roots in the neighborhood.

1937

Pyramid Club Founded

The Pyramid Club is established at 1517 Girard Avenue for the "cultural, civic and social advancement of Negroes in Philadelphia," becoming the city's leading African American social club and supporting artists like Dox Thrash.

1940s-1950s

Artistic Innovation Era

Dox Thrash co-invents the revolutionary Carborundum printmaking process. His Sharswood studio becomes a center for artistic experimentation, with support from organizations like the Pyramid Club fostering a vibrant creative community.

1950s-1960s

Women's Medical College & Professional Growth

The Women's Medical College nearby trains pioneering women doctors, many of whom serve the Sharswood community. The neighborhood becomes home to a growing professional African American middle class.

1964-1968

Cecil B. Moore & Civil Rights Victory

Civil rights lawyer Cecil B. Moore leads the historic seven-month picket of Girard College, the longest sustained civil rights action in Philadelphia history. The campaign succeeds in desegregating the institution in 1968.

1965

Thrash's Artistic Legacy

Dox Thrash passes away, leaving behind a transformative artistic legacy. His techniques and vision continue to influence printmakers and artists worldwide, while his Sharswood home remains a symbol of creative excellence.

1960s-1970s

Black Power & Arts Movement

Sharswood becomes a center for Black Power organizing and the Black Arts Movement. Community centers, cultural organizations, and activists use the neighborhood as a base for social change and artistic expression.

1970s-1980s

Community Institutions & Cultural Life

Athletic Recreation Center, Black Panthers organizing, UNIA activities, community photography documentation by photographers like Mosley, and vibrant church life continue to define Sharswood's character.

1990s-2010s

Historic Recognition Grows

Growing recognition of Sharswood's historical significance, with scholars, artists, and community leaders working to document and preserve the neighborhood's remarkable contributions to American culture and civil rights.

2020

Preservation Renaissance

Beech Community Services acquires the Dox Thrash House, beginning comprehensive restoration efforts. This marks the start of a new era of historic preservation and community celebration.

2025

Historic District Campaign

Launch of the comprehensive campaign to designate Sharswood as Philadelphia's newest Historic District, honoring Dox Thrash, Octavius Catto, Cecil B. Moore, and the countless community members who made this neighborhood a center of African American achievement.

Civil Rights Heroes of Sharswood

⚾ Octavius Catto (1839-1871)

Pioneering civil rights activist who organized the Philadelphia Pythians baseball team, fought for streetcar desegregation, and championed equal education. Murdered on Election Day 1871 while defending voting rights. His legacy lives on in Sharswood's commitment to equality and justice.

⚖️ Cecil B. Moore (1915-1979)

Militant civil rights lawyer who led the historic seven-month picket of Girard College, achieving its desegregation in 1968. His house and legacy remain central to Sharswood's identity as a neighborhood of resistance and victory against segregation.

🎨 Dox Thrash (1893-1965)

Master printmaker who co-invented the Carborundum process, revolutionizing American art. His Sharswood studio became a beacon of artistic innovation, documenting African American life with unprecedented depth and technical brilliance.

🏛️ The Pyramid Club

Founded in 1937 at 1517 Girard Avenue, this was Philadelphia's leading African American social club, dedicated to "cultural, civic and social advancement." The Pyramid Club supported artists like Dox Thrash and fostered community leadership.

🏥 Women's Medical Pioneers

The nearby Women's Medical College trained pioneering women doctors who served the Sharswood community. These medical professionals broke barriers and provided crucial healthcare to neighborhood families.

✊ Black Power Movement

Sharswood served as an organizing hub for Black Power activists, Black Panthers, and the Black Arts Movement. Community centers and cultural spaces nurtured radical political thought and artistic expression.
